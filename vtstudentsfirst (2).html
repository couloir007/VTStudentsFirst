The Issue

Tuitioning is how rural Vermont delivers public education.

In Vermont, over 90 towns have no public high school of their own. Tuitioning — the right of families to choose an approved school and have tuition paid by their town — is how these communities provide public education. For generations, students in Danville, Sutton, Newark, and dozens of other rural towns have attended St. Johnsbury Academy, Lyndon Institute, Burr and Burton Academy, Thetford Academy, and other regional schools through this system.

Now, three legislative proposals threaten to dismantle this system — not by debating its merits, but by redesigning how districts are structured so that family choice disappears by default.

The Chair of the House Education Committee has explicitly stated that his goal is to eliminate school choice. Members of the committee have even referred to independent schools as "vendors" — a term that reveals a fundamental misunderstanding of how rural Vermont's education system actually works. We are taking them at their word — and organizing to stop it.

This is not about ideology. It is about equity. Tuitioning is Vermont's rural education delivery mechanism. Protecting it means protecting the same standard of access that every Vermont student deserves.

Act 73 Phase 2 — Forced Consolidation

Would restructure how districts are organized, replacing family-level tuitioning with board-controlled "designation" — meaning a school board assigns students to a school rather than families choosing one. Under this model, only four historic academies — Burr and Burton, Thetford, St. Johnsbury, and Lyndon Institute — could be designated at all, and even they face forced enrollment reductions that would cut programs and staff.

H.777 — Elimination of Tuitioning

Would eliminate public tuition funding for any independent school that does not follow public school rules (EQS). Because most independent schools operate under the rigorous Independent School Rules (Series 2200), this bill would effectively ban students from attending them with public tuition dollars.

H.813 — Imposition of Public Rules

Would mandate that all independent schools follow public school regulations — removing the operational flexibility that makes these schools effective. This is a thinly-veiled attempt to homogenize Vermont's educational ecosystem and strip the independence that generations of families have valued.

What's at Stake

This affects more than
one school or one town.

🎓

Program Access

CTE pathways, AP courses, arts programs, dual enrollment, and world languages exist at regional academies that smaller consolidated schools could not replicate. Reassignment ends access overnight.

🚌

Rural Commutes

For families in the Northeast Kingdom and other rural regions, alternative schools can mean 45–90 minute one-way commutes — a barrier that disproportionately harms working families.

🏗️

Workforce Pipelines

Regional academies partner with local employers, hospitals, and trades. Disrupting enrollment disrupts workforce development pipelines that rural Vermont's economy depends on.

👨‍👩‍👧

Family Stability

Families choose Vermont communities in part because of school access. Eliminating choice creates relocation risk — and rural Vermont cannot afford to lose young families.

♿

Student Supports

Students with IEPs and special needs are often well-served by academy environments with robust support infrastructure. Any reassignment policy must preserve — and strengthen — these outcomes.

🏘️

Community Anchors

In many NEK towns, the local academy is the largest employer and the social center of the region. Enrollment changes destabilize economies, not just schools.

Our Position

Local flexibility.
Statewide accountability.

We are not asking for special treatment. We are asking Vermont to protect the mechanism it already uses to deliver public education in rural communities — while holding all schools receiving public dollars to clear, enforceable standards.

1

Preserve family-level tuitioning access

Non-operating towns must retain the right to provide students with genuine school choice, not board-only designation that eliminates options. Tuitioning is not a loophole — it is Vermont's rural education policy.

2

Protect rural equity — not special treatment

Tuitioning is how Vermont ensures equity for students in towns without a high school. Defending it is defending the same standard of access that every Vermont student deserves.

3

Require accountability from all receiving schools

We support enforceable guardrails: transparency in spending, student support obligations, nondiscrimination requirements, and regular outcomes reporting for any school receiving public tuition dollars.

4

Prevent destabilizing disruption

Any governance changes must include safeguards against sudden reassignments, loss of established programs, or changes that undermine workforce pipelines and community economic stability.

5

Governance cannot be a backdoor to end tuitioning

If consolidation or restructuring proceeds, statute must explicitly prevent governance changes from being used to eliminate family choice in non-operating towns. The protection must be durable.

Our Accountability Framework

We support guardrails.
Real ones.

Protecting tuitioning doesn't mean writing a blank check. We believe any school receiving Vermont public tuition dollars should meet clear, enforceable standards. This is our position — not a concession.

📊

Transparent Financial Reporting

Schools receiving public tuition dollars must disclose how those funds are used, with annual public reporting to the State.

♿

Student Support Obligations

Receiving schools must provide or fully fund special education services for tuitioning students — no exceptions, no cost-shifting to sending towns.

⚖️

Nondiscrimination Requirements

All tuition-receiving schools must comply with state and federal nondiscrimination law as a condition of continued eligibility.

📈

Outcomes Reporting

Graduation rates, post-secondary outcomes, and student performance data must be reported publicly and annually for all tuitioning students.

Setting the Record Straight

Myth vs. Fact.

These talking points address the misinformation circulating in Montpelier and at school board meetings. Use them when speaking with neighbors, legislators, or the press.

Myth

Independent schools are draining the Education Fund.

Fact

Publicly funded independent school students make up only 4% of the student population and just 3% of Education Fund spending. Districts that tuition students to independent schools consistently spend $184 less per pupil ($13,763) than the state average ($13,947). Independent schools receive no state construction aid, saving taxpayers millions while public schools face over $6 billion in deferred maintenance.

Myth

Independent schools have no oversight or accountability.

Fact

Independent schools are governed by Rule Series 2200 — nearly twice the length of public school standards. Unlike public schools, independent schools must undergo formal review and re-approval by the Agency of Education every five years. They are subject to the same background checks, mandated reporter laws, and statewide assessment requirements as public schools.

Myth

Independent schools aren't equitable and don't serve all students.

Fact

Under Act 173, independent schools that receive public tuition dollars are required to accommodate students with special education needs. Discrimination based on any protected class is illegal under state and federal law. Many independent schools provide specialized social and emotional environments where neurodiverse students thrive — offering flexibility that large public systems often cannot match.

Myth

Consolidation will save money and improve outcomes.

Fact

Districts with tuitioning and school choice already spend less per pupil than the state average — not more. Any restructuring that eliminates family-level tuitioning would discard a cost-effective delivery model that rural Vermont has built over generations, while imposing new administrative costs and forcing long commutes on students in towns that never had a local public school to begin with. The issue isn't the structure of districts — it's whether families retain the right to choose.
