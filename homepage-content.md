## PARAGRAPH 2 — issue_section

**field_section_label**
```
The Issue
```

**field_section_headline**
```
Tuitioning is how rural Vermont delivers <em>public education.</em>
```

**field_intro_text**
```
In Vermont, over 90 towns have no public high school of their own. Tuitioning — the right of families to choose an approved school and have tuition paid by their town — is how these communities provide public education. For generations, students in Danville, Sutton, Newark, and dozens of other rural towns have attended St. Johnsbury Academy, Lyndon Institute, Burr and Burton Academy, Thetford Academy, and other regional schools through this system. Now, three legislative proposals threaten to dismantle it — not by debating its merits, but by redesigning district structures so that family choice disappears by default.
```

**field_cards** (3 × card_item paragraphs)

**Card 1**
- field_card_title: `Act 73 Phase 2 — Loss of Family Choice`
- field_card_body: `Would restructure how districts are organized, replacing family-level tuitioning with board-controlled "designation." Only four historic academies could be designated at all, and even they face forced enrollment reductions that would cut programs and staff.`
- field_card_variant: `urgent`

**Card 2**
- field_card_title: `H.777 — Elimination of Tuitioning`
- field_card_body: `Would eliminate public tuition funding for any independent school that does not follow public school rules (EQS). Because most independent schools operate under the rigorous Independent School Rules (Series 2200), this bill would effectively ban students from attending them with public tuition dollars.`
- field_card_variant: `urgent`

**Card 3**
- field_card_title: `H.813 — Imposition of Public Rules`
- field_card_body: `Would mandate that all independent schools follow public school regulations — removing the operational flexibility that makes these schools effective and stripping the independence generations of Vermont families have valued.`
- field_card_variant: `urgent`

## PARAGRAPH 3 — stakes_section

**field_section_label**
```
The Stakes
```

**field_section_headline**
```
What happens if rural school choice <em>disappears?</em>
```

**field_intro_text**
```
Dismantling the tuitioning system wouldn't just limit choices — it would trigger an educational and economic crisis for rural Vermont communities that have built their towns around these schools.
```

**field_cards** (4 × card_item paragraphs)

**Card 1**
- field_card_title: `Property Tax Shock`
- field_card_body: `Non-operating towns currently avoid the capital cost of building and maintaining a high school. Forced consolidation would put construction and operations costs onto local property tax bills — with no state construction aid to offset it.`

**Card 2**
- field_card_title: `Loss of Specialized Programs`
- field_card_body: `Students currently access programs in arts, technology, agriculture, and sciences that small single districts can't sustain. Enrollment caps and forced reassignments would eliminate these options entirely.`

**Card 3**
- field_card_title: `Economic Anchor at Risk`
- field_card_body: `Historic academies are economic engines for their regions — drawing families, staff, and visitors who fuel local businesses, housing, and tax revenue. Stripping them of public tuition funding threatens the vitality of the towns around them.`

**Card 4**
- field_card_title: `Two-Hour Commutes for Rural Kids`
- field_card_body: `For families in remote areas, choice isn't a luxury — it's the only way to ensure their children aren't assigned to a school an hour away. Attendance zones in rural Vermont mean long bus rides, not neighborhood schools.`

```
Our Position
```

**field_section_headline**
```
Local flexibility. <em>Statewide accountability.</em>
```

**field_intro_text**
```
We are not asking for special treatment. We are asking Vermont to protect the mechanism it already uses to deliver public education in rural communities — while holding all schools receiving public dollars to clear, enforceable standards.
```

**field_position_items** (5 × position_item paragraphs)

**Position 1**
- field_position_title: `Preserve family-level tuitioning access`
- field_position_body: `Non-operating towns must retain the right to provide students with genuine school choice — not board-only designation that eliminates options. Tuitioning is not a loophole. It is Vermont's rural education policy.`

**Position 2**
- field_position_title: `Protect rural equity — not special treatment`
- field_position_body: `Tuitioning is how Vermont ensures equity for students in towns without a high school. Defending it is defending the same standard of access that every Vermont student deserves.`

**Position 3**
- field_position_title: `Require accountability from all receiving schools`
- field_position_body: `We support enforceable guardrails: transparency in spending, student support obligations under Act 173, nondiscrimination requirements, and regular outcomes reporting for any school receiving public tuition dollars.`

**Position 4**
- field_position_title: `Prevent destabilizing disruption`
- field_position_body: `Any governance changes must include safeguards against sudden reassignments, loss of established programs, or changes that undermine workforce pipelines and community economic stability.`

**Position 5**
- field_position_title: `Governance cannot become a backdoor to end tuitioning`
- field_position_body: `If restructuring proceeds, statute must explicitly prevent governance changes from being used to eliminate family choice in non-operating towns. The protection must be durable — not subject to future board discretion.`

## PARAGRAPH 6 — myth_section

**field_section_label**
```
Setting the Record Straight
```

**field_section_headline**
```
Myth vs. <em>Fact.</em>
```

**field_cards** (4 × card_item paragraphs — title = myth, body = fact)

**Card 1**
- field_card_title: `Independent schools are draining the Education Fund.`
- field_card_body: `Publicly funded independent school students make up only <strong>4% of the student population</strong> and just <strong>3% of Education Fund spending</strong>. Districts with tuitioning spend <strong>$184 less per pupil</strong> than the state average ($13,763 vs. $13,947). Independent schools receive <strong>no state construction aid</strong>, while public schools carry over $6 billion in deferred maintenance.`

**Card 2**
- field_card_title: `Independent schools have no oversight or accountability.`
- field_card_body: `Independent schools are governed by <strong>Rule Series 2200</strong> — a standard nearly twice the length of public school requirements — and must be formally re-approved by the Agency of Education <strong>every five years</strong>. They are subject to the same background checks, mandated reporter laws, and statewide assessment requirements as public schools.`

**Card 3**
- field_card_title: `Independent schools don't serve students with disabilities.`
- field_card_body: `Under <strong>Act 173</strong>, independent schools receiving public tuition dollars are required to accommodate students with special education needs. Many provide specialized environments where neurodiverse students thrive in ways that larger consolidated schools cannot match.`

**Card 4**
- field_card_title: `Restructuring districts will save money and improve outcomes.`
- field_card_body: `Districts with tuitioning already spend <strong>less per pupil than the state average</strong>. Any change that eliminates family-level choice would discard a cost-effective delivery model built over generations — without evidence that consolidation improves student outcomes.`
