The Issue

Tuitioning is how rural Vermont delivers public education.

In Vermont, over 90 towns have no public high school of their own. Tuitioning — the right of families to choose an approved school and have tuition paid by their town — is how these communities provide public education.

For generations, this system has allowed students in Danville, Sutton, Newark, and dozens of other rural towns to attend St. Johnsbury Academy, Lyndon Institute, and other regional schools — receiving a high-quality education without a 45-minute bus ride to a consolidated school they never chose.

Proposed legislation would shift control away from families toward school board "designation" — effectively ending choice and forcing students into reassignments that could mean longer commutes, lost programs, and disruption to entire communities.

This is not about ideology. It's about equity. Protecting tuitioning means protecting the way Vermont has always served its most rural students.

What's changing

Pending legislation would allow boards to "designate" a receiving school — replacing family-level tuitioning access with a single board-controlled assignment, with no guarantee families can choose differently.

Who is affected

Students and families in non-operating towns who currently choose SJA, Lyndon Institute, or equivalent schools — and the communities and employers who depend on local academies as regional anchors.

What we're asking for

Explicit statutory protection for tuitioning access in non-operating towns, plus clear statewide accountability guardrails for any school receiving public tuition dollars.

What's at Stake

This affects more than
one school or one town.

🎓

Program Access

CTE pathways, AP courses, arts programs, dual enrollment, and world languages exist at regional academies that smaller consolidated schools could not replicate. Reassignment ends access overnight.

🚌

Rural Commutes

For families in the Northeast Kingdom and other rural regions, alternative schools can mean 45–90 minute one-way commutes — a barrier that disproportionately harms working families.

🏗️

Workforce Pipelines

Regional academies partner with local employers, hospitals, and trades. Disrupting enrollment disrupts workforce development pipelines that rural Vermont's economy depends on.

👨‍👩‍👧

Family Stability

Families choose Vermont communities in part because of school access. Eliminating choice creates relocation risk — and rural Vermont cannot afford to lose young families.

♿

Student Supports

Students with IEPs and special needs are often well-served by academy environments with robust support infrastructure. Any reassignment policy must preserve — and strengthen — these outcomes.

🏘️

Community Anchors

In many NEK towns, the local academy is the largest employer and the social center of the region. Enrollment changes destabilize economies, not just schools.

Our Position

Local flexibility.
Statewide accountability.

We are not asking for special treatment. We are asking Vermont to protect the mechanism it already uses to deliver public education in rural communities — while holding all schools receiving public dollars to clear, enforceable standards.

1

Preserve family-level tuitioning access

Non-operating towns must retain the right to provide students with genuine school choice, not board-only designation that eliminates options. Tuitioning is not a loophole — it is Vermont's rural education policy.

2

Protect rural equity — not special treatment

Tuitioning is how Vermont ensures equity for students in towns without a high school. Defending it is defending the same standard of access that every Vermont student deserves.

3

Require accountability from all receiving schools

We support enforceable guardrails: transparency in spending, student support obligations, nondiscrimination requirements, and regular outcomes reporting for any school receiving public tuition dollars.

4

Prevent destabilizing disruption

Any governance changes must include safeguards against sudden reassignments, loss of established programs, or changes that undermine workforce pipelines and community economic stability.

5

Governance cannot be a backdoor to end tuitioning

If consolidation or restructuring proceeds, statute must explicitly prevent governance changes from being used to eliminate family choice in non-operating towns. The protection must be durable.

Our Accountability Framework

We support guardrails.
Real ones.

Protecting tuitioning doesn't mean writing a blank check. We believe any school receiving Vermont public tuition dollars should meet clear, enforceable standards. This is our position — not a concession.

📊

Transparent Financial Reporting

Schools receiving public tuition dollars must disclose how those funds are used, with annual public reporting to the State.

♿

Student Support Obligations

Receiving schools must provide or fully fund special education services for tuitioning students — no exceptions, no cost-shifting to sending towns.

⚖️

Nondiscrimination Requirements

All tuition-receiving schools must comply with state and federal nondiscrimination law as a condition of continued eligibility.

📈

Outcomes Reporting

Graduation rates, post-secondary outcomes, and student performance data must be reported publicly and annually for all tuitioning students.
